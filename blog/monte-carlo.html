Monte Carlo Simulations

I've been wondering what Monte Carlo simulations are for more than a while now. But it never really struck me until recently to wiki it up. I was rather surprised by what I found - I didn't think that something with such an imposing name could be something that I could actually implement. In fact, I was so surprised that I decided to try writing a program right away.

The program in question is one that attempts to estimate the value of $ \pi $. The figure that inspired me to try this out can also be found on the wiki page.

In short, here's the way the method works:

  1. Create a bunch of random points in the range $[0,1) \times [0,1)$ uniformly
  2. Check which points fall inside the unit circle and which ones don't
  3. Quite clearly, the ratio of the two numbers gives you the ratio of the two areas (since the distribution of random numbers is uniform!)
  4. Since the area of the unit circle is simply $ \pi $, the ratio we just procured is one-fourth the value of $ \pi $, and therefore we need to multiply it by 4 to get the actual value.

And that's really all that's there to a Monte Carlo simulation. Any simulation that does its work by choosing a random sample from some known distribution and then counting how many items lie in an area of interest qualifies as a Monte Carlo simulation!

Being the perfectionist that I am, I was pretty dissatisfied with the above implementation because it is rather inefficient: double-for-loop! So I decided to give numpy a spin for optimization. Vector operations are blazing fast compared to for loops in python.



This turned out to be much better than the naive implementation. For instance, the naive implementation took around 2.8 seconds for $ 10^6 $ points. On the other hand, the vectorized implementation managed the same in just 0.15 seconds!

Okay, so now that I had a faster implementation, I could actually start asking some questions. One of them was this: How is it better to use more points? I expect the error to reduce, but by how much? In other words, I wanted an idea of how the error scales with the number of points. Since the number of points is also linked to the time taken for computation, I would indirectly be asking how long I would have to wait (and the amount of computing resource I would need) in order to achieve a certain accuracy.

As it turns out, this was a fairly interesting exercise. First I made a simple program along the lines of the two previous ones that tried to plot the general trend in the error as numbers grew.



This turned out the way I expected. The law of decreasing returns. I kind of guessed that the decrease was exponential, but there was no way to know until I did a more detailed analysis.



Much later, I realised that the error calculation that I was doing was kind of silly because it only did the experiment once, for each number-of-points. This isn't useful because there's always a chance that the simulation with $ 10^4 $ points will get lucky and hit closer to 3.14159 than the simulation with $ 10^7 $ points, say. Of course, it's much less likely to happen, but it might. And I'm doing the experiment only once, so I'll never know.

A better way, therefore, is to repeat the experiment several times for each given number-of-points. Then, the standard deviation in the values so obtained would give me a measure of how far off from the value of pi the estimates usually go.



Naturally, this gave a far more pleasing result.



As to whether or not the curve was exponential was easy to determine. All I had to do was to take the same data and make a semilog graph out of it, with log scaling in the x-axis. An exponential curve would then become linear.




The plot looked fairly linear, but it still had a slight curve to it. But I satisfied myself for the moment and moved on to an even more interesting question.
