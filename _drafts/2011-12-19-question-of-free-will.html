So I was discussing with a friend something I had earlier touched upon. The so-called "scientific theory of God". And it struck me as strange that as this theory wove into its more scientific intricacies, we seemed to have to question our very freedom of movement, of breathing, of choice, indeed of the very writing of this sentence.

The question is this - how deterministic is the world? The universe, given its current condition, consists of about 10^64 particles (or so some estimates say). Nevertheless, the number is less important than the fact that it is finite. Indeed, the number can be even bigger 10^(10^64) even, provided of course, that it remains within such a realm as countability.

Having said this, let me proceed to elaborate - assume for a moment that at some given instant of time we knew the exact positions and momenta of all the world's particles. We do not, naturally, demand that we know the last absolute detail - we shall leave electronic energies and transitions aside in this discussion. But assume for a moment, that we knew the position and velocity of all particles at a given instant. Of course, the forces are well defined - gravitational, electromagnetic, whatever - and calculable. Meaning we would now have the position at a given instant, the velocity at a given instant, and the acceleration as a function of time of every single particle in the universe.

It is, naturally, a lot of data - far beyond what our miniscule brain (that comprises not even a trillionth of this data) even fathom, let alone process. But suppose that we could hold and process such data. Would we not then, have determinism? What I mean to say is that we now have a seemingly complete set of equations - solving which is merely a matter of computation - a task that is well beyond what we would imagine to be the horizon of impossibility - yet, only a matter of computational difficulty - and therefore in theory, one would have determinism.

But that means that I now know the exact trajectory of every single particle in the universe - every single one - including, for example, the ones that make up your body, or even mine. Now comes the big jump. Do we really need to "make" that "assumption"? Is it not enough to say that nature knows her laws, and will guide everything, without so much as toeing the line? Is it not enough to say that things will follow their course, regardless of the so-called "assumptions" we make? For who are we, but just simple - almost arbitrary - collections of particles that make up the whole? And so it goes.

Have we not, in the course of this discourse then, lost our freedom? Just like that? Have we not just proved that nature has it all planned out - predetermined - whether we like it or not? Is it not clear now, that we are just another bunch of atoms, intrinsically lifeless, that just happen to exhibit certain patterns of behaviour and having certain properties - eating, sleeping, reproduction - to name a few? For where, if anywhere, have I gone wrong in this examination of existence?

Perhaps the mistake I am making is in assuming that we in fact do have freedom. Perhaps that is the actual mistake, the actual reason for this curious contradiction. Is it not possible that each of these processes - the evolution of life, the evolution of behaviour, the evolution of action - was all known (albeit not to us) from the start? Is it not remotely possible that even as I type the keys of my keyboard, I do so not through free will, rather, because the neurons in my brain are firing electric pulses, the intensity and time-distribution of which in turn were preordained by the continuous firing of other neighbouring neurons, thus having resulted in a change in the very "fabric" of the cell - the so-called evolution of "memory" through "learning" - which in their own turn, were "propelled" by these long chain-like molecules - (deoxy or not) ribonucleic acid - by an elaborate set of meticulously calculated and precisely ordered chemical interactions, which at their base, relied on purely physical principles of collision? Perhaps I really have no freedom, and even the very "belief" that I have it is something that was predetermined - foretold - by some non-existent entity, whose might and knowledge we simply cannot fathom, simply because we, as systems of particles, do not possess such a property.

But no! I want to find a way out of this madness, for my mind repels the very thought of such an idea. The horrific truth revealed is too much to bear. Perhaps the mistake I made is in not accounting for those quantum effects. For chemical reactions, are, after all, caused by electron transfer - the electronic transitions and energies, therefore, become suddenly important! Yes! Perhaps we are just glitches, errors - caused by the whimsical tendencies of quantum mechanics, whose motives are, and will always remain, a complete mystery - in this otherwise unstained universe. For the universe seems to follow the simple laws of relativistic mechanics, for the most part. It is only in such domains wherein the interaction of elements is markedly intense that blemishes such as ourselves have the audacity to appear - if only by the indeterminism that is characteristic of the physics that guides such reactions - and find solace in that everyone else is also but a blemish, a mistake, a defect.

How much more soothing a parallel have I drawn? For I remarked once the lack of our freedom, and then the lack of our orderliness - our conformance to physical norm. We jut out as anomalies - existential paradoxes, dilemmas. I leave things here, in want of a better answer...

This was a piece I'd written in the early hours of the 8th of November. I had intended to publish it immediately, but a friend insisted that I withhold it until such time as I could provide a more concrete answer. And finally, an answer have I found.

For not more than a week ago, I happened to come across this very question in Feynman's Lectures on Physics, Volume 3.

Feynman answered the question rather well. He first pointed out that even from a classical viewpoint, there was never much "predictability", considering that the amounts of data concerned was too large. Secondly, even if we had the capability to hold and compute such large volumes of data, we would still need to know accurately the positions and velocities of every single particle in the universe. Now it is impossible, practically, to determine anything to an arbitrary degree of accuracy. But quantum mechanics demands that there be a limit on this accuracy. Indeed, we cannot know the positions and momenta to any degree of accuracy beyond h-cross over two:
$\Delta x \Delta p \geqslant h/4\pi$
But assume for a moment that even Heisenberg's principle were not true. Indeed, it has only held together as a sort of axiom, we have no proof for it, and it may fail some day, if we happen to find an experimental setup that can surpass such an accuracy. Assume for a moment that you know, to an arbitrary degree of accuracy, the positions and momenta of all particles. Then, Feynman says that there exists a time (less than the time that it would take to verbally state that accuracy) within which our system of particles has near-zero determinism. That is to say: suppose we know the position and momentum to an accuracy of a billion billionths of a unit. Then within a fraction of a second [citation needed], we would find that the errors in the current positions and velocities of the particles have magnified to such an extent (as a result of intermolecular collisions) that we no longer have any meaningful knowledge of their future trajectories. I believe this is what is also known as "Chaos theory" - one of our professors gave us a very brief introduction, but I cannot say I have anything beyond the most superficial idea of the subject.

Therefore, even from a classical viewpoint, we never had any "determinism" in this world. Nor must we call ourselves "anomalies" or "existential dilemmas". We are a part of the universe, and we will follow its natural laws - albeit laws that amount to chaos. May I end with another quote (clichéd though it may by now be)?

You know the thing about Chaos? It's fair. - The Joker

Well, if my own understanding of quantum mechanics is correct, then that is exactly the point I was trying to make: nature (or the "universe" as you put it) herself does not know the exact positions and momenta of her particles. Sounds absurd I know, but true to the best of my knowledge.
That is in fact the flaw with Newtonian mechanics: while we can't accurately make measurements, nature herself ought to know where and what she is doing - and therefore, given the current state, the future is deterministic.
But quantum mechanics requires (again, I think) that nature herself does not know this. She has deliberately made herself "chaotic" (if that is the right word to use - it may have different meanings in other contexts!) to the extent that she does not understand herself. Hence the breakdown of the input-output function and the existence of free will!
